# Media Literacy

For content commits: blog drafts (Leaflet.pub posts, Ghost blog), presentation scripts (e.g. NSTP group work), documentation, or any writing that makes factual or citable claims before it gets published.

## Fact-checking

- Every factual claim in the draft should trace back to a source you could actually point to. Flag any claim that's currently unsourced ("this needs a citation" rather than silently trusting it).
- Prefer primary sources (government data, original studies, the org's own statement) over aggregator summaries when verifying a claim.
- For statistics or numbers, check the number is current — figures on fast-moving topics (population, funding amounts, casualty counts, policy status) go stale.

## Bias & framing check

- For contested or political topics, check whether the draft presents one side's framing as settled fact. This doesn't mean stripping out a point of view the piece intentionally takes — it means being deliberate about it rather than accidental.
- Check headline/summary framing against the body: a common failure mode is a more inflammatory headline than the evidence in the piece supports.
- Where multiple credible sources disagree, the draft should say so rather than picking one silently.

## Gen-AI-era specific failure modes

- **Hallucinated citations**: if any section was AI-drafted or AI-assisted, verify that every cited source actually exists and says what the draft claims it says — fabricated citations that look plausible are the single most common failure mode here.
- **Fabricated quotes/stats**: check any quoted text against the original; don't let a paraphrase drift into something rendered as a direct quote.
- **Laundered claims**: watch for a claim that's stated with more confidence than any of the underlying sources actually support — a common pattern when a claim passes through an AI summarization step.
- **Agentic-web currency**: if the piece references a tool, API, protocol, or product's current state (pricing, availability, version, endpoint), verify it's still accurate now rather than trusting what a model "remembers" — these details go stale fast and are exactly the kind of thing worth a quick current search before publishing.

## Further reading / background

- Compile a short further-reading list of primary sources so readers can cross-check claims themselves rather than taking the piece's word for it — this is especially worth doing for anything touching land rights, SDGs, or other topics where the Regalian Doctrine / Philippine legal context matters and readers may want the primary text.
- Note any claim in the draft that you weren't able to verify, rather than quietly cutting it or quietly leaving it in — surface it as an open item for the user to resolve.
